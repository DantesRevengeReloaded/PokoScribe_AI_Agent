### Summary of the Article: "Antecedents and Consequences of Employee Engagement Revisited" by Alan M. Saks (2019)

#### **1. Information about Psychology**
Employee engagement is a psychological construct rooted in Kahn’s (1990) concept of personal engagement, which involves the physical, cognitive, and emotional investment of employees in their work roles. Schaufeli et al. (2002) further defined engagement as a positive work-related state characterized by vigor, dedication, and absorption. Saks (2006) expanded this by distinguishing between **job engagement** (engagement in one’s specific job role) and **organization engagement** (engagement as a member of the organization). Engagement is influenced by psychological factors such as meaningfulness, safety, and availability, which are shaped by job characteristics, social support, and organizational resources.

#### **2. Information about Job Satisfaction**
Job satisfaction is a key consequence of employee engagement. Engaged employees are more likely to experience higher levels of job satisfaction due to the fulfillment they derive from their work. Saks (2006) found that both job engagement and organization engagement significantly predict job satisfaction. The study also revealed that engagement mediates the relationship between job characteristics, perceived organizational support (POS), and job satisfaction. This suggests that when employees feel supported and their jobs are designed to be meaningful, they are more likely to be engaged and, consequently, satisfied with their work.

#### **3. Information about Leadership**
Leadership plays a critical role in fostering employee engagement. Transformational leadership, in particular, has been shown to positively influence engagement by inspiring employees and creating a supportive work environment. Saks (2006) highlighted that perceived supervisor support (PSS) and perceived organizational support (POS) are significant antecedents of engagement. Leaders who provide recognition, rewards, and opportunities for development can enhance employee engagement. Additionally, ethical and authentic leadership styles have been linked to higher levels of engagement, as they build trust and psychological safety among employees.

#### **4. Information about the Impact of the Above on Work Performance and Productivity**
Employee engagement has a direct and positive impact on work performance and productivity. Engaged employees are more likely to exhibit organizational citizenship behaviors (OCB), such as helping colleagues and going beyond their job requirements. They also demonstrate higher levels of task performance and extra-role performance. Saks (2006) found that engagement mediates the relationship between job characteristics, POS, and performance outcomes. Furthermore, engagement reduces turnover intentions, which contributes to organizational stability and productivity. Organizations that foster engagement through supportive leadership, fair practices, and meaningful job design are likely to see improvements in overall performance.

#### **5. Other Relevant Information**
- **Job Characteristics**: Skill variety, autonomy, and feedback are key job characteristics that drive engagement. Skill variety, in particular, was found to be the strongest predictor of job engagement.
- **Perceived Organizational Support (POS)**: POS is a critical antecedent of both job and organization engagement. Employees who feel supported by their organization are more likely to engage deeply in their roles.
- **Single-Item Measures**: Single-item measures of job and organization engagement were found to be effective and practical for assessing engagement, providing organizations with a quick and easy way to monitor employee engagement levels.
- **Mediating Role of Engagement**: Engagement mediates the relationship between antecedents (e.g., job characteristics, POS) and consequences (e.g., job satisfaction, performance, turnover intentions).

#### **6. Summary of Discussion**
The study revisits Saks’ (2006) model of employee engagement and confirms its validity using the Utrecht Work Engagement Scale (UWES) and single-item measures. The findings suggest that skill variety and POS are the most significant predictors of job engagement. Engagement, in turn, predicts job satisfaction, organizational commitment, OCB, and reduced turnover intentions. The study also highlights the importance of leadership and job design in fostering engagement. A revised model of engagement is proposed, incorporating additional antecedents such as job demands, opportunities for learning, and leadership styles. The study concludes that organizations can enhance engagement by focusing on skill variety, POS, and supportive leadership, which will ultimately improve performance and productivity.

Leadership plays a critical role in either fostering or mitigating workplace bullying.
